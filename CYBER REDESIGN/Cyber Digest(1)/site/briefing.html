Inside the Volt Typhoon resurgence: 90 days of dwell, pivot and living off the land.

The operators returned to OT-adjacent telco infrastructure in March. We reconstruct the access chain — from edge router compromise to AD persistence — and map it against MITRE ATT&CK v16.

Between March 11 and May 14, Cyber Digest tracked at least seven intrusions consistent with the operator known publicly as Volt Typhoon. The targeting pattern — telco edge, transport-adjacent OT, a small number of regional water utilities — has not changed. The tradecraft, in places, has.

01 · The access chain

In four of the seven incidents, initial access traced to an unpatched edge router exposing a management interface on a non-standard port. In the remaining three, the operators reused credentials previously stolen from an MSP — credentials that had not been rotated despite a 2025 disclosure. Neither path is novel. What's new is the speed of pivot: median time from access to first hands-on-keyboard activity collapsed from 14 hours to under 90 minutes.

Once inside, operators relied almost exclusively on certutil, wmic, netsh and built-in PowerShell modules. There is no novel malware to fingerprint. That, of course, is the point.

02 · Dwell & lateral movement

The mean dwell window across the seven cases was 47 days. In two of the longest, operators appear to have deliberately limited activity to working hours in the target time zone — a small behavioural detail that consistently defeats simple anomaly detection.

"There was no exotic implant. The most dangerous tool on the network was Notepad." — Incident responder, EU telco, August 2024

Lateral movement followed a familiar shape: harvest service-account credentials from a domain-joined jump box, pivot via SMB to a backup server, exfil staged data over an already-allowed HTTPS path to a compromised content-delivery edge.

03 · Persistence

In every case, persistence resolved to one of three primitives: a scheduled task masquerading as a Windows Update servicer, a WMI event subscription bound to system idle, or a registry Run key under a HKLM hive that defenders rarely audit. None of these are clever. All of them work.

Why this matters
The campaign is interesting not because it is novel but because it isn't. The tradecraft that worked against telco operators in 2024 is still working today. The asymmetry isn't the adversary's skill — it's defenders' patch cadence.

04 · Detection guidance

Below is a high-confidence Sigma rule covering the LSASS-access pattern observed in five of seven cases. It assumes Sysmon with the SwiftOnSecurity baseline.

sigma · lsass-access-via-rundll32.yml↳ Copy
# Volt Typhoon — LSASS access via signed binary
title: Suspicious LSASS access via rundll32
id:    5b81-2026-04-vt-01
status: experimental
logsource:
  product: windows
  service: sysmon
detection:
  selection:
    EventID: 10
    TargetImage|endswith: '\lsass.exe'
    SourceImage|endswith: '\rundll32.exe'
    GrantedAccess: '0x1410'
  condition: selection
level: high
tags:
  - attack.credential_access
  - attack.t1003.001
